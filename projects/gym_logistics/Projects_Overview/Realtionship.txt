Overview:

* Trainer <--> Branch (Many-to-Many): Unlike physical equipment, a personal trainer is flexible.
One trainer can pick up shifts or train clients across multiple physical branches. At the same time, any 
single branch location employs multiple trainers. Django manages this by creating a hidden third table 
behind the scenes to link their IDs together.

* Trainer <--> TrainerSalary (one to one):This is a strict, isolated relationship. One trainer can only 
have one active salary payroll profile to protect sensitive banking data. That financial record can never
be shared with or mapped to another trainer.

Trainer --> GymClass (One-to-Many): A single trainer can be assigned to teach multiple classes throughout 
the week (e.g., Yoga on Monday, HIIT on Wednesday). If a trainer leaves the gym, your code uses on_delete=models.SET_NULL,
meaning the class stays on the schedule but safely updates to say "No Trainer Assigned" instead of deleting 
the whole schedule slot.

# Overview:

** Branch --> GymClass (One to Many): A physical branch location hosts a structured schedule of many classes
. A specific Zumba session at 9:00 AM on a Tuesday happens at only one specific branch.

** Member & GymClass --> ClassBooking (Associative/Join Table): A member can book many different classes a week, and a single 
gym class can have many members reserve a spot. The ClassBooking table acts as the bridge. It records who booked what class and 
when.Member & Gym

** The Guardrail: Your code includes a strict database rule: unique_together = ('member', 'gym_class'). 
This makes it physically impossible for a member to click "book" twice and accidentally steal two seats
in the exact same class timeslot.

** ClassBooking <--> AttendanceLog (One to One): Once a booking reservation is successfully created, it is paried
with exactly one attendence ledger row. This tracking sheet exists solely to mark wheather taht specific member actually showed up
Present or ABSENT
